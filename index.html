Key Contract Principles

Scope of Work

Clearly defines all work to be performed, materials to be used, and project specifications to prevent disputes.

Pricing Structure

Outlines payment schedule, deposit limits, and how variations will be priced and approved.

Project Timeline

Includes start and completion dates, milestones, and provisions for delays beyond either party's control.

Warranties & Guarantees

Details statutory warranties, defect liability periods, and how issues will be rectified post-completion.

Frequently Asked Questions

Apartment & Unit Contracts

Yes, standard domestic building contracts apply to apartments and units, but with some additional considerations. Multi-unit developments often have:

  • Strata title implications that affect contract terms
  • Shared infrastructure requirements
  • Additional compliance with body corporate rules
  • Different insurance requirements compared to standalone homes

Always ensure your contract specifically addresses these multi-unit considerations.

Apartment contracts should specifically include:

  • Clear definition of boundaries between private and common property
  • Provisions for access to common areas during construction
  • Details about noise and disruption management in shared buildings
  • Specific warranties for shared infrastructure (elevators, fire systems, etc.)
  • Clarification on who maintains temporary construction facilities

Defect liability in apartments follows the same statutory warranty periods as standalone homes (typically 6 years for structural defects), but with additional complexity:

  • The builder must coordinate with the body corporate for common area repairs
  • Defects affecting multiple units may require a coordinated repair approach
  • Access arrangements for repairs must consider privacy of other residents
  • Your contract should specify how defect notifications will be handled in a multi-unit context

Builder Liquidation Protections

If your builder enters liquidation:

  • The contract is typically terminated automatically
  • You'll need to engage a new builder to complete the work
  • Any unspent deposit money should be recoverable through insurance
  • Progress payments for incomplete work may be claimable
  • Statutory warranties may still apply to completed work

Your first step should be contacting your state's building authority and consulting a construction lawyer.

Protections vary by state but typically include:

  • Domestic Building Insurance: Covers deposits and incomplete work (up to policy limits)
  • QBCC Home Warranty Scheme: In Queensland, provides up to $200,000 coverage for incomplete work
  • Statutory Trust Accounts: Some states require progress payments to be held in trust
  • Deposit Limits: Maximum 5% deposit reduces exposure

Always verify your builder's insurance is current before signing and making payments.

Immediate steps to take:

  1. Stop all payments to the builder immediately
  2. Contact your insurer to start a claim for incomplete work
  3. Notify your state building authority (e.g., QBCC in Queensland)
  4. Secure the site to prevent damage or theft
  5. Consult a construction lawyer about your options
  6. Document everything including contract, payments, and work completed
  7. Get quotes from other builders to complete the project

Time is critical - most insurance claims have strict notification deadlines.
